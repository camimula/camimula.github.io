===== Summary =====
- In software development, tests are more valuable than implementation
	- Stop writing unit tests out of inertia
	- Consider automating tests as close to E2E as possible
- SQLite Core, Imperative Shell
	- Snapshot API responses and necessary data from server-based DBs into SQLite, and have the application reference SQLite
	- The boundary between unit tests and integration tests becomes very thin
	- Property-based testing becomes more E2E-oriented and realistic

===== 0. Scope of Discussion =====
The following discussion is written with business software in mind, which presumably constitutes the majority of software in the world. I believe the fundamental ideas do not differ greatly for software built for software, but I ask for your understanding where inconsistencies may arise.

Furthermore, I recognize that software with UIs—so-called frontend—falls outside the scope of this discussion. In other words, the target here is what we call the backend. While this may seem quite limited, given the current movement toward shifting more responsibility to the backend, as seen with htmx, it likely covers a substantial area of interest.

===== 1. The Value of Testing =====
Software is more a means than an end. In that sense, it is only natural to ask whether the objective has been achieved, and only tests can answer that question. One must note that how something was achieved (architecture, code) does not guarantee what was achieved. Therefore, I believe testing should be the first thing considered.

In essence, this is TDD. However, I feel that TDD has been distorted into an implementation-level methodology. What matters is asking whether the objective has been achieved, and on that basis, favoring the most direct method free of extraneous context—meaning the tests we should write are E2E tests. E2E tests can directly express what should be achieved, and as a bonus, in theory they should never break except when requirements change, making them longer-lived than the implementation.

Unfortunately, E2E tests face harsh barriers: cost, constraints, brittleness, and so on. The progression of compromise toward smaller scopes—integration tests, then unit tests—is the context of the test pyramid. I suspect this ultimately became accepted as the doctrine that thickening the unit test layer is sufficient.

I use the term "doctrine" pejoratively because unit tests easily degenerate into tests of implementation. As stated earlier, implementation does not guarantee the achievement of objectives, so one must remain perpetually skeptical of the value of unit tests. When writing unit tests, strategies such as maximizing effectiveness through Functional Core, Imperative Shell should accompany them.

Ultimately, one should maintain the perspective that we are making compromises from E2E testing. Those compromises are reasonable, but how far to compromise must be continuously re-examined.

As a digression, let me add a remark on an underexplored effect of testing. Tests serve as instructions for what should be achieved and as guarantees of what has been achieved. I believe that treating this as a contract and leaving the rest to individual discretion is one ideally desirable development style. I feel that entrusting the freedom of detailed design to individual discretion is a form of providing incentives in development—could this not be called truly "inclusive" development? One might enjoy the analogy with Acemoglu's theory, but that is not the main topic, so I will leave it at that.

Additionally, this should serve as a prescription for AI-assisted coding, where review burden is a growing concern. On the non-functional side, most workplaces already achieve adequate or at least acceptable guarantees through security scanning and load testing. Depending on how much one is willing to accept, it should not be impossible to substantially—or in some cases entirely—eliminate the burden of the review process.

===== 2. SQLite Core, Imperative Shell =====
I mentioned earlier that E2E is a compromise, but let me revise that slightly. Even if we could solve the problems of E2E testing, there remains one barrier that is nearly insurmountable: execution speed, which stems from the physical constraint of the network. The remedy would be parallel execution, but when attempting to reproduce vast precondition states via property-based testing to increase E2E test coverage, scaling becomes rather impractical.

So, it may also be worthwhile to reverse the thinking and examine how close we can bring fast-executing unit tests to E2E. This is what I propose as "SQLite Core, Imperative Shell." The claim is quite simple: just insert SQLite between the application and the server-based RDB, as follows:

Server-based RDB → SQLite ←→ Application

Snapshot the necessary tables and rows from the server-based RDB into SQLite, and have the application issue queries against SQLite. When snapshotting, if only simple WHERE clauses are permitted between the server-based RDB and SQLite, behavioral differences from the production environment become negligible. In other words, unit tests can be brought closer to the level of integration tests.

The benefits of this approach are not limited to improving test effectiveness. The ability to write filters and aggregations within the application in SQL is also a powerful advantage. Because SQL is relationally complete, it can handle most use cases adequately. The role of code becomes primarily that of glue—control flow, value transformations not supported by SQL, and other fairly limited tasks. Moreover, there will likely be cases where delegating to SQLite's years of optimization yields better performance than writing code by hand.

As mentioned, thanks to its mathematical foundation and long track record of adoption, SQL is an extremely stable data access interface. SQLite in particular has guaranteed support through 2050, ensuring that the core of your logic becomes very robust. Even as the surrounding environment evolves rapidly, if the core remains guaranteed, keeping up with updates should become considerably easier.

===== 3. Known Limitations =====
There is no silver bullet, and this approach has its limitations. Let me state my honest opinions on what I can see at this point.

3.1 The Problem of Assuming RDB
Let me address why I have been deliberately writing "server-based RDB." The world's data sources are not limited to RDBs—there are NoSQL databases, external APIs (JSON), and more. The question of whether to put all that data into SQLite is a fair one.

Honestly, if the architecture depends heavily on external APIs, I must admit this approach is largely powerless. In such architectures, the problem essentially becomes one of mocking. If logic is involved, it would be far more sensible to adopt and test with Functional Core, Imperative Shell. Conversely, that is all there is to it—if promotion is not possible, it simply comes back to the original question of where to place the compromise.

Regarding the diversity of data sources, I would argue that RDBs suffice for all but the most unusual non-functional requirements. NewSQL now exists, and the write-scaling problem is essentially solved. In the first place, services that actually scale to that degree are exceedingly rare, and the scenarios where one would adopt an alternative DB at the cost of additional management overhead and forgoing transaction support are quite limited.

As for diversity in data structure, I perceive the problem as nonexistent. SQLite supports JSON, so it is hard to imagine this becoming a fatal issue. Furthermore, one should note that the reality of schemaless is merely lazy evaluation of schemas. Data whose schema truly cannot be defined in advance is difficult to use programmatically in the first place, and being quite exceptional, it is unavoidable that individual handling is required.

3.2 The Write Problem
In the methodology described above, I deliberately avoided addressing writes. I discussed Arrange and Act but must admit I did not address the target of Assert.

For example, if the target of Assert is an API server response (JSON, etc.), there is no problem at all. However, when the target is the state of data in the DB, a problem arises: should we look at SQLite's data or the server-based RDB's data?

Naturally, the correct answer is to check the server-based RDB. Under the assumption that INSERT and UPDATE behavior does not differ significantly across databases, one possible compromise is to place target tables for writes within SQLite and then reflect the written data back to the server-based RDB.

Even so, guarantees regarding whether INSERT/UPDATE behavior matches that of the server-based RDB, as well as handling of conflicts, will be insufficient. I must admit that the value as unit tests inevitably diminishes, and in that sense, claiming to have reached integration test level was a somewhat dishonest assertion. This is the greatest structural deficiency. Nevertheless, even if one uses a real DB only for writes or simply accepts the limitation, the approach remains highly effective.

3.3 Still Not Reaching E2E
I advocated valuing E2E, but the proposed method only achieves limited promotion of unit tests. One might consider an approach that replaces the server-based RDB with SQLite for speed gains, but that is simply mocking. It lowers test value while introducing problems with dialect absorption and behavioral differences. This is an unavoidable point of compromise. Ultimately, E2E testing remains essential. That said, integration-level tests have relatively high capacity to express requirements, and if that layer is thick enough, it is also a fact that it can be sufficiently effective.

3.4 Immaturity of the Testing Ecosystem
I called for property-based testing for coverage. While the ecosystem for property-based testing of functions is mature, it is admittedly immature for SQL. Unfortunately, this will require either waiting for the ecosystem to mature or building it yourself.

Let me offer some casual thoughts on building a property-based testing foundation. Even when doing property-based testing at the integration test level, the unit for creating data is likely not individual tables but somewhat larger units (such as members/users). For creating data consistently across multiple tables, the Factory pattern is a reasonable candidate. I believe it would be worthwhile to look to FactoryBot, which has already achieved remarkable results in this area.

In my view, the concept of understanding domains and mapping them to code, popularized by DDD and others, is an achievement of object-oriented programming. However, the methodology has bloated into something bewilderingly complex, and it is hard to deny that it has become a burden on development. Ultimately, there seems to be a gap when it comes to mapping concepts to functionality, but the mapping should be much closer when it comes to data. Lately, I have been fantasizing that perhaps this was something that should have grown within the context of testing.

===== 4. Conclusion =====
In Section 1, I discussed the importance of tests that are close to E2E. In Section 2, I discussed techniques for bringing unit tests closer to E2E using SQLite, and in Section 3, I also pointed out the limitations of this approach.

I have proposed a methodology called "SQLite Core, Imperative Shell." I think it is an interesting idea worth considering—I can only hope it is not a reinvention of the wheel. As the name suggests, Functional Core, Imperative Shell and SQLite are the major inspirations behind this concept. I would like to express my gratitude to their respective creators, Gary Bernhardt and Dr. Richard Hipp.

That said, personally, more than promoting a methodology, I would rather broaden the inquiry into the value of testing. The reason I began writing this article is that I recently encountered the opinion that "template generation is an area where AI excels, so it should also write test code." Whether to have AI write tests is a matter of individual judgment, but I believe that testing, as discussed above, cannot possibly be a tedious task of filling in templates.

Therefore, I would first like to reiterate that generating unit tests out of inertia—which is likely the cause of viewing tests as templates—produces no value. As I have tried to show both explicitly and implicitly, testing, despite its importance, is a largely untapped vein with considerable potential for creative work, and I wish to assert this once more.

I would be delighted if this serves as an occasion for my esteemed readers to explore the possibilities of testing once again.
